FRBSF Chart Builder — Project Description
What Is This?
The FRBSF Chart Builder is an AI-powered web application built for the Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco's Economic Research division. It enables economists and research analysts to rapidly create, customize, and publish publication-quality economic charts from FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data) datasets or uploaded CSV/Excel files — with AI assistance at every step.

The tool combines a visual canvas editor (drag-and-drop chart elements), a natural language AI assistant (powered by AWS Bedrock Claude Sonnet 4.5), and Vision AI (OpenCV + Bedrock Vision) that can analyze a reference chart image and replicate its styling automatically. The result is a complete chart authoring workflow: from raw data ingestion to polished, branded output ready for FRBSF publications, policy briefs, and presentations.

The Use Case
Economic research teams at the Federal Reserve regularly produce charts for Economic Letters, working papers, policy briefings, and public-facing web content. The typical workflow involves:

Downloading data from FRED or internal sources
Building charts in Excel, matplotlib, R/ggplot2, or proprietary tools
Manually matching FRBSF brand guidelines (colors, fonts, layout conventions)
Adding annotations like recession bands, policy rate targets, and event markers
Writing executive summaries that contextualize the data
Exporting in multiple formats for different audiences (web, print, code-reproducible)
Each of these steps is manual, time-consuming, and error-prone. Formatting consistency across publications is hard to maintain. When a chart needs revision — a different date range, a new annotation, a chart type change — the analyst often starts from scratch.

The FRBSF Chart Builder collapses this entire pipeline into a single interactive session.

Business Value
Time savings. What typically takes 30-60 minutes of manual chart creation, formatting, and export can be done in under 5 minutes. Paste a FRED URL, get a chart. Upload a reference image, get matching styling. Ask the AI to add annotations. Generate a summary. Export.

Brand consistency. Every chart produced follows FRBSF visual standards. Colors, fonts, axis formatting, and layout are applied automatically — either from defaults or by mimicking an approved reference image. This eliminates the "every analyst has their own style" problem.

Reproducibility. Charts export as standalone Python (matplotlib) and R (ggplot2) scripts with embedded data. Any colleague can re-run the script to reproduce the exact chart without needing the original tool, data files, or configuration. This matters for peer review, audit trails, and publication workflows.

Accessibility of AI. The natural language AI assistant lowers the barrier for analysts who aren't comfortable with code. Instead of writing matplotlib commands to add a horizontal reference line at 2% with a dotted red style, they type "add a dotted red line at 2% labeled inflation target." The AI handles the translation.

Institutional knowledge capture. Executive summaries are auto-generated with trend analysis, peak/trough identification, forward-looking outlook, and economist-perspective interpretation. This provides a structured starting point that analysts can refine, ensuring key analytical dimensions aren't overlooked.

What It Can Do
Data Ingestion

Paste any FRED URL (e.g., https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/GDP) to automatically download the series and generate a chart
Upload CSV or Excel files with automatic date detection and long-format pivoting
Optionally attach a reference chart image (screenshot of a FRED page, a published chart, or an internal template) — the Vision AI extracts colors, chart type, annotations, gridlines, axis formatting, and data table structure, then applies that styling to your data
Interactive Chart Editing

Drag-and-drop canvas: move title, legend entries, annotations, and data table anywhere
Chart types: line, bar, area, and mixed (per-series type selection)
Full axis control: labels, ranges, scales (linear/log), Y-axis formatting (auto, integer, percent, decimal), line widths, font sizes
Annotations: horizontal reference lines, vertical event lines, recession-style shaded bands, floating text labels — all addable via UI buttons or AI commands
Data table: transposed time-series table with series-colored text, computed columns (e.g., month-over-month change), and drag-to-resize handles on the canvas
Right-click context menus for font customization, label renaming, and annotation deletion
Resizable VS Code-style split panes for all screen sections
AI Assistant

Natural language chart modifications: "change to area chart," "make the y-axis show percentages," "add a vertical band for the 2020 recession," "change the title to GDP Growth"
Data Q&A: "what was the peak value?", "when did the series reach its lowest point?", "compare the trend before and after 2020"
Executive summary generation and updates: auto-generates structured summaries with trend analysis, peaks/troughs, predictions, and economist perspective; supports appending additional analysis via chat
Undo support for AI-driven chart changes
Export

Python: standalone matplotlib script with embedded data, date-aware x-axis, area fills, annotations, and FRBSF branding (zip with requirements.txt)
R: standalone ggplot2 script with embedded data (zip with install_packages.R)
PDF: high-resolution chart image (300 DPI) with executive summary and FRBSF branding
All exports work without saving a project first — export directly from the current canvas
Project Management

Save and load charts to a local SQLite database
Resume work across sessions with full state restoration including dataset reload
Why Economic Analysts Should Use It
It speaks your language. The AI assistant understands economic chart conventions. Ask for "recession bands" and it creates vertical shaded regions. Ask for an "inflation target line" and it adds a horizontal dotted line. It knows what FRED series are, what annotations economists typically use, and how to structure an executive summary with the right analytical framework.

It eliminates formatting drudgery. Matching a published chart's exact styling — colors, line widths, gridline patterns, axis formatting, annotation placement — is tedious manual work. Upload a screenshot of the target chart and the Vision AI does it for you. This is especially valuable when updating charts for recurring publications where visual consistency across editions matters.

It bridges the code gap. Not every economist writes Python or R fluently. The Chart Builder lets you create a chart visually, then export it as clean, runnable code. This means the chart is both visually authored and computationally reproducible — satisfying both the "I need it now" and "it needs to be reproducible" requirements.

It keeps you in flow. Instead of switching between FRED's website, a spreadsheet, a coding environment, and a document editor, everything happens in one interface. Data ingestion, chart creation, annotation, summary writing, and export are all in the same session.

It produces publication-ready output. The PDF export includes FRBSF branding, high-resolution chart rendering, and the executive summary — ready to attach to an Economic Letter draft or share with a policy team. The Python/R exports are ready for a research appendix or replication package.

How the Output Will Be Used
The outputs from the FRBSF Chart Builder feed directly into the economic research publication pipeline:

Economic Letters and Working Papers: Charts are exported as PDF or high-resolution images and embedded in publications. The accompanying Python/R scripts go into replication packages that allow peer reviewers and future researchers to reproduce the analysis.

Policy Briefings and Presentations: Charts with executive summaries are used in internal briefings for bank leadership and FOMC preparation. The auto-generated summary provides a structured analytical narrative that economists refine for their specific audience.

Public-Facing Web Content: FRBSF publishes economic data visualizations on its website. The consistent branding and export capabilities ensure charts meet web publication standards.

Cross-Team Collaboration: Saved projects and exported code scripts allow analysts to share work with colleagues. One economist can create a chart, save the project, and another can load it, modify it, and re-export — maintaining continuity across team members and over time.

Data Exploration and Analysis: Before committing to a publication chart, analysts use the tool for rapid exploration — trying different chart types, date ranges, and annotations to find the most effective way to communicate a data story. The AI Q&A feature helps surface insights (peaks, troughs, trends) that inform the narrative.

Archival and Audit: Exported Python/R scripts with embedded data serve as a permanent, self-contained record of how a chart was produced. This supports institutional requirements for reproducibility and transparency in published research.
